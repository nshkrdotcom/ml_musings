# 1. Core mental model: ML optimization is calculus on an absurdly large shape

A modern loss function is usually a scalar field:

[
L(\theta): \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
]

where (\theta) may contain millions, billions, or more parameters. Optimization means moving through this high-dimensional space to reduce (L). The basic calculus objects are:

| Object                 | Meaning                                | Optimization role                                                                         |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Gradient (\nabla L)    | Direction of steepest local increase   | Step opposite it                                                                          |
| Hessian (H=\nabla^2 L) | Local curvature matrix                 | Tells whether the landscape bends up/down/flat                                            |
| Eigenvalues of (H)     | Curvature along special axes           | Positive = bowl direction, negative = escape direction, near-zero = flat/sloppy direction |
| Critical point         | (\nabla L=0)                           | Could be minimum, maximum, saddle, or degenerate plateau                                  |
| Morse index            | Number of negative Hessian eigenvalues | Measures “how saddle-like” a critical point is                                            |

The big intuition shift: **in high dimensions, “local minimum” is not the only important object. Saddles, ridges, flat manifolds, connected valleys, and nearly-zero-curvature sheets dominate the picture.**

# 2. High-dimensional geometry: why 2D intuition breaks

In 2D, a loss landscape looks like hills and valleys. In high dimensions, that picture is misleading.

**Volume concentrates near boundaries.** In high-dimensional balls, most volume is near the outer shell. Random directions are nearly orthogonal. A “nearby neighborhood” is mostly not where low-dimensional intuition expects it to be.

**Most critical points are saddles, not minima.** Random matrix and spin-glass arguments suggest that, as dimension grows, the probability that every Hessian eigenvalue is positive becomes tiny. A point with mixed curvature is much more likely than a true isolated local minimum.

**Flat directions are everywhere.** Neural nets are overparameterized, symmetric, and redundant. That creates enormous subspaces where changing parameters barely changes the function. A trained model is often not sitting in a neat bowl; it may be on a broad, nearly flat sheet or valley.

**Distance becomes strange.** In high dimensions, many points are almost equally far apart. Euclidean geometry can become a poor proxy for functional difference. Two parameter vectors may be far in Euclidean distance but represent similar functions, while a small step in a sharp direction may radically change loss.

# 3. Loss-landscape cheat sheet

## Local shapes

| Shape               | Hessian signature                                      | Optimizer behavior                                           |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Sharp minimum       | Many large positive eigenvalues                        | Easy to locate, often brittle/generalizes worse              |
| Flat minimum/basin  | Many small positive or near-zero eigenvalues           | Often better generalization; SGD noise tends to prefer these |
| Saddle              | Positive and negative eigenvalues                      | Negative-curvature directions allow escape                   |
| Plateau             | Gradient small, Hessian small                          | Slow progress; momentum/noise/adaptive methods help          |
| Ridge               | High curvature in one direction, flat/curved elsewhere | Step size must be controlled                                 |
| Valley/tunnel       | Low-loss corridor through parameter space              | Explains mode connectivity and ensembling paths              |
| Degenerate manifold | Continuum of equivalent solutions                      | Common because of symmetries and overparameterization        |

## Practical optimizer interpretation

**SGD** is not just gradient descent. Its minibatch noise helps it jiggle out of saddles and may bias it toward wider basins.

**Momentum** helps carry the trajectory through ravines and across shallow barriers.

**Adam/RMSProp-style methods** rescale coordinates, which is useful when curvature varies wildly across dimensions.

**Newton/natural-gradient/quasi-Newton methods** try to account for curvature or geometry, but full curvature is usually too expensive at modern scale.

**SAM and SAM-like methods** explicitly seek flat neighborhoods rather than just low pointwise loss.

# 4. Exotic mechanisms: tunnels, twists, saddles, and “not-really-minima”

## 4.1 Mode connectivity: good solutions are often connected

One of the most counterintuitive discoveries is that two independently trained neural networks may appear to land in different “minima,” but there can be a low-loss path connecting them. This is called **mode connectivity**. Recent work continues extending this from curves to richer surfaces and even input-space analogues; IBM’s 2025 work, for example, explores connectivity using Bézier surfaces rather than only paths. ([IBM Research][1])

Interpretation: the loss landscape is not a desert full of isolated pits. It may be more like a high-dimensional cave system: many chambers connected by tunnels, saddles, ramps, and sheets.

## 4.2 Entropic barriers: tunnels can exist but still be hard to traverse

A subtle newer point: even if low-loss tunnels exist, SGD may not naturally explore them. A 2025 paper frames this as **entropic confinement**: optimization can remain trapped in one basin not because the path is blocked by high loss, but because curvature and noise make the connecting region statistically unlikely to enter. ([arXiv][2])

This is important because “connected minima” does not automatically mean “optimizer freely roams among them.”

## 4.3 Saddles as bridges, not just obstacles

Classically, saddles are treated as bad places where optimization stalls. But from a topological viewpoint, saddles can be **gateways** that connect basins. Morse-theoretic work connects Hessian index to loss-landscape connectedness and argues that some optimizer-found “solutions” may be high-index saddle-like structures rather than genuine isolated minima. ([Proceedings of Machine Learning Research][3])

That flips the story: a saddle is not merely a trap; in high dimensions, it can be a pass, tunnel mouth, or junction.

## 4.4 Flatness is not just low curvature; it is volume

A flat basin can contain vastly more parameter-space volume than a sharp basin. So even if a sharp basin has slightly lower loss at one point, SGD may be more likely to find the broad basin. This is sometimes described as an **entropic bias** toward flat solutions.

SAM makes this explicit by minimizing worst-case loss in a neighborhood. Recent ICLR 2025 work found that SAM can select flatter minima late in training, and that applying SAM for only a few final epochs can achieve much of the effect of full SAM training. ([arXiv][4])

## 4.5 Edge of stability: optimization can surf near instability

Classical gradient descent theory says the learning rate should be small enough that the loss decreases smoothly. But neural networks often train at the **edge of stability**, where the largest Hessian eigenvalue hovers near the stability threshold (2/\eta). The training loss can oscillate short-term while still decreasing long-term. This phenomenon has remained active in 2024–2025 research, including analysis of deep linear networks beyond the edge of stability. ([OpenReview][5])

This suggests optimizers may deliberately or implicitly exploit near-instability to move through sharp regions and settle into flatter ones.

## 4.6 Multifractal landscapes: nested roughness at many scales

A 2025 Nature Communications paper models deep-learning loss landscapes as **multifractal**, aiming to explain clustered degenerate minima, multiscale structure, edge-of-stability behavior, anomalous diffusion, and edge-of-chaos-like dynamics under one framework. ([Nature][6])

This is a promising idea because neural loss surfaces often look smooth at one scale and rugged at another. A multifractal view treats the landscape less like a single mountain range and more like nested terrain: cracks within valleys within basins within plateaus.

# 5. The latest innovation clusters

## A. Topological Data Analysis: measuring the landscape’s “shape”

Instead of only looking at gradients and Hessians, TDA studies how **sublevel sets** change:

[
{\theta : L(\theta) \leq c}
]

as (c) increases.

Tools include:

| Tool                  | What it measures                                     |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Merge trees           | How low-loss components appear and merge             |
| Persistent homology   | Which holes/tunnels/components survive across scales |
| Persistence diagrams  | Lifetimes of topological features                    |
| Morse-Smale complexes | Basins and gradient-flow partitions                  |

Recent work such as *Evaluating Loss Landscapes from a Topology Perspective* uses topological tools to make landscape comparison more quantitative rather than relying only on 2D visualizations. ([arXiv][7])

Why this matters: topology can detect “tunnels,” multiple basins, saddle mergers, and ruggedness in ways that Hessian-at-a-point analysis misses.

Promising innovation: **topology-aware training diagnostics**. For example, instead of asking only “is validation loss going down?”, one could ask whether the sublevel topology is becoming simpler, more connected, or less saddle-fragmented.

## B. Morse theory: saddles as topology-changing events

Morse theory says that the topology of sublevel sets changes when you pass critical points. In ML terms:

* minima create new low-loss components;
* saddles merge components or create tunnels;
* maxima cap off regions;
* Hessian index tells what kind of topological event occurred.

This gives a rigorous language for “basins connected by tunnels.” It also suggests that some architectures work better because they reshape the topology of the loss, reducing harmful barriers or increasing low-loss connectivity.

## C. Riemannian optimization: stop pretending the space is flat

Standard gradient descent treats parameter space as Euclidean. But many ML objects naturally live on curved spaces:

| Manifold                                  | ML use                                        |
| ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Sphere                                    | normalized embeddings, directional parameters |
| Stiefel manifold                          | orthogonal weight constraints                 |
| Grassmann manifold                        | subspace learning                             |
| SPD manifold                              | covariance matrices, metrics                  |
| Hyperbolic space                          | trees, hierarchies, graphs                    |
| Probability simplex/statistical manifolds | distributions and classifiers                 |

Riemannian optimization replaces straight-line steps with geometry-aware moves: geodesics, retractions, exponential maps, parallel transport, and metric-adjusted gradients.

Recent work continues pushing this into large-scale settings. A 2025 accelerated natural-gradient framework for parametric manifold optimization combines Riemannian flow ideas, Hessian-driven damping, projected momentum, Kronecker approximations, and metrics such as Fisher-Rao and Wasserstein-2. ([arXiv][8])

Potential payoff: optimizers that handle twisted valleys better because they measure distance in the right geometry rather than raw parameter coordinates.

## D. Information geometry: optimize the function, not the coordinates

Information geometry treats model predictions as points on a statistical manifold. The central object is the **Fisher Information Matrix**. Natural gradient descent uses:

[
\Delta \theta \propto -F(\theta)^{-1}\nabla L(\theta)
]

This makes the update more invariant to reparameterization. In simple language: two parameter changes that produce the same change in the model’s probability distribution should be treated similarly, even if their raw Euclidean sizes differ.

Recent work continues connecting Adam-like adaptive methods and natural-gradient views, including arguments that Adam can be interpreted through diagonal empirical Fisher approximations under certain assumptions. ([OpenReview][9])

Innovation direction: combine cheap adaptive optimizers with better approximate information geometry, especially for transformers where exact Fisher methods are too expensive.

## E. Optimal transport and Wasserstein geometry: optimization as moving probability mass

Optimal transport gives a geometry on probability distributions. Instead of following a single point (\theta_t), one can study a distribution of parameters, particles, functions, or predictions evolving over time.

Wasserstein gradient flows are increasingly used to connect optimization, sampling, generative modeling, and mean-field neural-network training. Recent work studies Wasserstein proximal algorithms and neural-network-projected Wasserstein gradient dynamics, including cases beyond geodesic convexity and numerical schemes for Wasserstein flows. ([OpenReview][10])

Useful metaphor: instead of a ball rolling downhill, imagine a fluid flowing through a cave system. It can split, merge, diffuse, and pass through multiple channels.

Promising innovation: **population-based optimizers** that use particle distributions to explore multiple valleys and communicate through transport geometry.

## F. Symplectic and Hamiltonian optimization: momentum as geometry

Momentum methods resemble physical systems. Symplectic geometry is the math of Hamiltonian dynamics, where position and momentum evolve together.

Recent work on conformal symplectic optimization frames neural-network training as a conformal Hamiltonian system and uses structure-preserving integrators to transfer long-term stability into update rules, especially in reinforcement-learning settings. ([arXiv][11])

Why this matters: naive discretization can inject artificial energy, cause oscillations, or destabilize training. Symplectic or conformal-symplectic schemes may better preserve the useful dynamics while adding controlled dissipation.

Innovation direction: optimizers that behave like “smart momentum”—fast through valleys, stable near curvature cliffs, less prone to chaotic spirals.

## G. Sharpness-aware training: directly targeting flat neighborhoods

SAM solves a local min-max problem: choose weights whose nearby neighborhood also has low loss. The family includes SAM, GSAM, Friendly SAM, Random SAM, efficient SAM variants, and trust-region-inspired variants.

Friendly SAM, for example, modifies the adversarial perturbation idea to better balance flatness-seeking and optimization efficiency. ([CVF Open Access][12])

This is one of the most practically actionable areas: SAM-like fine-tuning can often be tried without redesigning the architecture.

# 7. A more precise taxonomy of “exotic shapes”

## Twists

A twist means the steepest descent direction changes rapidly as you move. Euclidean gradient descent zigzags. Natural-gradient, K-FAC, quasi-Newton, or Riemannian methods can help by adapting to local geometry.

## Tunnels

A tunnel is a low-loss path or surface connecting regions that look separated in a low-dimensional slice. Mode connectivity and Bézier-surface connectivity are examples. ([IBM Research][1])

## Saddles as passes

A saddle with a few negative directions may be an escape route. A saddle with many negative directions may indicate high-energy instability. Morse index gives a way to classify these.

## Flat sheets

Because of overparameterization and symmetries, the “minimum” may be a broad manifold, not a point. Sharpness measured naively can be misleading unless symmetries and reparameterizations are handled.

## Fractal/multiscale roughness

The landscape may have large smooth valleys with smaller rough structures inside them. Multifractal models attempt to explain this nested behavior. ([Nature][6])

## Rotational flows

In adversarial learning, GANs, multi-agent RL, and some constrained games, the update field may not be a gradient of a single scalar potential. Then trajectories can spiral, cycle, or orbit instead of converging. This is why game optimization often needs extra-gradient, optimistic, consensus, or symplectic-inspired methods.

# 8. Practical implications for finding useful local minima

For modern ML, the goal is usually not “find a mathematically exact local minimum.” It is:

1. find a low-loss region;
2. avoid sharp brittle basins;
3. escape high-loss saddles;
4. exploit connected valleys;
5. end in a region that generalizes.

A good practical stack:

**Baseline:** SGD with momentum or AdamW.

**Flatness bias:** SAM or late-phase SAM fine-tuning.

**Curvature diagnostics:** top Hessian eigenvalue, trace approximations, sharpness measures, gradient noise scale.

**Topology diagnostics:** mode connectivity tests, low-loss path search, merge-tree/persistence-style analysis for smaller models.

**Geometry-aware methods:** natural gradient/K-FAC for models where feasible; Riemannian optimizers for constrained or geometric representations.

**Stability tuning:** watch for edge-of-stability behavior; do not assume short-term loss oscillations mean failure if long-term trend improves.

**Exploration:** use noise, cyclical learning rates, SWA, ensembles, or particle/population methods to sample wider connected regions.

# 9. The deepest takeaway

The old picture is:

> “Neural-network optimization is hard because the landscape has many bad local minima.”

The newer picture is closer to:

> “Neural-network optimization works because high-dimensional overparameterized landscapes contain vast connected low-loss regions, many saddles are escapable or connective, flat regions dominate useful volume, and the optimizer’s noise/geometry implicitly selects among these structures.”

The most promising future direction is hybrid: **topology to diagnose the global shape, information/Riemannian geometry to choose better local steps, stochastic/thermodynamic theory to control exploration, and symplectic/Hamiltonian ideas to stabilize momentum through twisting valleys.**
